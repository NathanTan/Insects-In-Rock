Introduction

Music is something of a miracle to me. What we know as sound is nothing more than inconceivably small variations in pressure traveling through the air. Ears and auditory organs vary tremendously across different walks of life but the basic idea is nearly universal in vertebrates. Over millions of years, organisms evolved structures that convert these tiny differences in air pressure into differences in fluid pressure, which is detected by sensory organs, which is converted into a signal and processed by the brain as a sound. I’m not a particularly religious individual; being a biology major, of course my beliefs around the topic of evolution are going to be grounded in science, but I can’t help but be extremely humbled by some of the ways that life evolved its many intricacies, like ears, and the ability to respond to sounds. What I find equally incredible is the fact that we as humans with our overwhelmingly large cerebral cortex, found ways to turn these weird things that we call sounds into a form of art, organizing them in time and putting them together in different ways based on their frequencies. Arranging sounds in innumerable different ways, creating combinations that some perceive as pleasant, and others do not, creating music about anything and everything, and creating entire cultures around different types of music. It’s an awesome part of what makes us human.

Humans have shared the planet with insects since before the species known as Homo sapiens had fully differentiated into its own species. Naturally, insects are going to show up in art created by humans, whether it’s thematically, metaphorically, or as a narrative device. There is no shortage of this in my personal favorite genre of music, Rock. I’ve got three songs that I want to share and discuss with you all today. Drones, by Rise Against. Butterflies and Hurricanes by Muse, and Everything Evil by Coheed and Cambria. Each one references an insect or a family of insects in a dramatically different way from the others. Each one gives us an insight into the long standing relationship between insects and humans.
