Introduction

Music is something of a miracle to me. What we know as sound is nothing more than inconceivably small variations in pressure traveling through the air. Ears and auditory organs vary tremendously across different walks of life but the basic idea is nearly universal in vertebrates. Over millions of years, organisms evolved structures that convert these tiny differences in air pressure into differences in fluid pressure, which is detected by sensory organs, which is converted into a signal and processed by the brain as a sound. I’m not a particularly religious individual; being a biology major, of course my beliefs around the topic of evolution are going to be grounded in science, but I can’t help but be extremely humbled by some of the ways that life evolved its many intricacies, like ears, and the ability to respond to sounds. What I find equally incredible is the fact that we as humans with our overwhelmingly large cerebral cortex, found ways to turn these weird things that we call sounds into a form of art, organizing them in time and putting them together in different ways based on their frequencies. Arranging sounds in innumerable different ways, creating combinations that some perceive as pleasant, and others do not, creating music about anything and everything, and creating entire cultures around different types of music. It’s an awesome part of what makes us human.

Humans have shared the planet with insects since before the species known as Homo sapiens had fully differentiated into its own species. Naturally, insects are going to show up in art created by humans, whether it’s thematically, metaphorically, or as a narrative device. There is no shortage of this in my personal favorite genre of music, Rock. I’ve got three songs that I want to share and discuss with you all today. Drones, by Rise Against. Butterflies and Hurricanes by Muse, and Everything Evil by Coheed and Cambria. Each one references an insect or a family of insects in a dramatically different way from the others. Each one gives us an insight into the long standing relationship between insects and humans.

Drones

Rise Against is a rock band from Chicago, Illinois that was formed in 1999. Along the spectrum of rock, they reside around the punk and harcore area. Their tracks focus on strong melodies and catchy hooks backed up by fast tempos, ripping guitar riffs, and pounding drum lines. As a band, they’re known for their advocacy, proudly supporting a several human rights organizations, and their lyrics are often built around social issues, and human rights. While they are often labeled as a political band pushing a certain ideology, their frontman Tim McIlrath encourages their listeners to look beyond the current political climate, noting that many of the problems they talk about in their songs are issues that have been around for a long time, and will likely continue to exist until a true progress is made.

Their song Drones comes to us from their 2006 album, The Sufferer and the Witness. At its core, it is a song about being forced to conform by a system that doesn’t care about you. Apart from the title though, there’s little to no direct reference to insects in the song, so what gives? Insects serve many roles in the art that people make, but the most common association made with bugs, at least in the U.S., is a negative one. Drones are male bees that serve no purpose other than to fertilize the queen of the colony. Many people hear the word “drone” and think of a mindless worker, often assuming drones are just worker bees that never stop what they’re doing. It’s this association that I wanted to point out. Drones in this song serve as a metaphor for a working class that has been marginalized, forgotten, and forced into mindlessness, doomed to work until the day that it dies. The song serves as a sort of overture for the working class in the U.S., for the people who live from paycheck to paycheck. It does a great job of outlining the negative association with bugs that people often make. Moreover, it’s an inaccurate association, most people couldn’t be bothered to look up what role a drone actually plays in a beehive. Still, the song’s message is a powerful one.

Biology of Apis mellifera

The european honey bee has a long history with humanity. It is a eusocial member of the order hymenoptera. Honey bees build nests where they house a colony. The colony consists of a single fertile queen, thousands of sterile females called workers and a small number of male drones whose sole purpose is to mate with the queen. A worker bee fits the standard bill for an insect very closely, with a body divided into a head, a thorax, and an abdomen. The head housing two large compound eyes and a pair of antennae. Honey bees have wings, which they beat at about 230 times per second in order to be able to fly. Workers in the colony have several jobs. Some leave the hive in search of flowers from which they are able to take nectar. They bring this nectar back to the hive and “process” it into honey, although it’s really just partially digested in a second stomach of theirs. Other workers have the task of raising young bees. Honey bees undergo a complete metamorphosis during their lives. Eggs are placed into cells of the honeycomb, where they hatched and are fed by worker bees. After some time, the cell is sealed with beeswax and the mature larva forms a pupa, from which a mature worker bee will emerge after a period of about a week. The way in which honey bees communicate has been extensively studied. Bees use a number of dances to convey information such as the location of a food source. Bees also rely heavily on the use of pheromones to communicate with regard to pretty much everything. When threatened, bees have a stinger which is actually a modified ovipositor. This stinger has barbs on the end and make it extremely difficult to remove from the target’s skin. When a bee uses its stinger, it leaves behind a the venom sac, and the bee loses a few vital organs in the process, dying within minutes of administering a sting. Most honey bees are temperate creatures, but they are distributed all over the world, able to be found on every continent except for antarctica. Throughout history, humanity has come to obtain a multitude of products from bees, including but not limited to, honey, beeswax, and royal jelly. The most profound economic contribution that bees make to humans though, is the pollination of billions of dollars’ worth of crops every single year.

Butterflies and Hurricanes

Hailing from the UK, Muse was formed in 1994. They perform a wide variety of alternative rock and are constantly experimenting and trying to find something new to do with their music. Their sound ranges from grungy, to electronic, to orchestral; they have fun with their music. The seeming spontaneity of their musical style manifests in their lyrics as well, drawing inspiration from just about anything, including a whole album based on George Orwell’s 1984.

Their 2003 album, Absolution deals with themes of introspection and personal achievement, as well as one’s relationship with the theological and a looming hypothetical apocalypse. The track Butterflies and Hurricanes exemplifies this perfectly. Thematically, it’s a very straightforward song. It’s a song about changing yourself for the better, self actualization; a sort of hype song that tells you “don’t worry, your time will come, everything’s going to be alright.” The song starts off calmly enough, with a simple guitar part and soft spoken vocals. As the verses progress, the drums gradually kick in and the music begins to swell with an orchestra in the background. Eventually the song explodes into a rock anthem that makes you feel like you’re in the middle of the hurricane. The song itself undergoes a transformation and really embodies the life cycle of a butterfly. Compared to Drones, this song is nearly polar opposite in the way it talks about insects. Butterflies and Hurricanes uses a butterfly’s metamorphosis as a very literal metaphor for self growth, self-actualization. It tells you to believe in yourself, that the hurricane will pass and that you will be better when you come out of it, that you’ll emerge as a transformed, beautiful butterfly version of your former self, if you will. It’s a very positive and optimistic view on a butterfly, one that respects it rather than looks down on it the way Drones does on the male bee.

Every Villian Is Lemons

Coming together in 1995, Coheed and Cambria is a progressive rock band with its roots in Nyack, New York; their style grounded firmly in the realm of rock and hard rock. Their earlier work was mostly progressive, breaking songs up into different, non-repeating sections, rather than the more more traditional “verse-chorus-verse-chorus” style of songwriting. Eight of their seven studio albums have been concept albums, each one serving as a chapter in an overarching sci-fi epic, and this is something that I absolutely love about them. Their lead singer, Claudio Sanchez, write novels and comic books, collectively called The Amory Wars, to help tell the story that these albums present. He has become something of a master of taking experiences from his own life, weaving them into components of this sci-fi adventure, and turning them into songs that are impressively relatable.

So what does a sci-fi epic have to do with insects? I’m glad you ask. The story of Coheed and Cambria’s first studio album The Second Stage Turbine Blade is, complicated, to say the least, especially when you have the context of the prequel albums to reference. In an overly simplified summary, the story’s main villain, Willhelm Ryan, unleashes swarms of genetically modified dragonflies. These dragonflies have stingers in place of where normal dragonflies would have claspers. These dragonflies were created with one purpose, to spread the Monstar Virus. Except that most people in this world are completely unaffected by the Monstar and these dragonflies instead are just a painful, irritating nuisance. The Monstar is meant for the titular characters, Coheed and Cambria who are actually very intricate robots, essentially, synthetic life forms. Upon exposure to the Monstar, the titular characters transform into monstrous, nightmarish versions of themselves. In this form, Coheed’s only purpose is to destroy everything in his path, and Cambria’s is to stop Coheed. Wilhelm Ryan did not realize that Coheed and Cambria’s creator installed this sort of failsafe in Cambria, and expected them both beat the planet that they were standing on into submission whereupon Wilhelm Ryan would swoop in, stop them both, and be hailed as a savior. Ryan’s plan fails, and *spoiler alert* both of the titular characters actually end up killing one another. The rest of the series follows their son and his quest to stop Wilhelm Ryan.

Believe it or not, all of that happens in the song Everything Evil. The song more or less opens up as Wilhelm Ryan is releasing the dragonflies and the line from the song’s chorus, “evolve! Monstar, do to me the things I never wanted done.” is very much referring to Coheed undergoing his transformation into a mindless, all-destructive version of himself. What I personally find humorous is where the symbolism of the dragonflies came from. As a child, Claudio Sanchez was terrified of dragonflies. He thought they had massive stingers on the ends of their tails and reacted to them the way that many children react to bees and wasps. It wasn’t until a presentation in a class he took in college that he learned that they were virtually harmless. He gained an appreciation for them and ended up incorporating them into the story of the Amory Wars as well as a lot of the band’s iconography. This complicated relationship between Sanchez and dragonflies is another great example of most people’s relationships with insects. Many people fear them or are disgusted by them, but they do not seek to learn more about them and learn to gain a proper appreciation for them. It’s something that i really like about our entomology class, it very much works to educate us and learn about how important they are. After all, they’ve been around much longer than we have, and realistically, they’ll be here long after we are not.
